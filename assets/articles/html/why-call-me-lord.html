Why Do You Call Me Lord?

A searching article on surrender, obedience, and the difference between naming Christ as Lord and truly living under His rule.

A Question of Alignment

There is a question that does not echo from the outside, but from within the chambers of the soul—a question that pierces deeper than doctrine, deeper than ritual, deeper even than belief: “Why do you call Me Lord?”

It is not a question of vocabulary. It is a question of alignment. For many have learned to say the word “Lord” with their lips, yet their lives whisper a different allegiance. The tongue declares surrender, but the will remains enthroned.

The Illusion of Reverence

There exists a form of reverence that is hollow—beautiful in sound, yet empty in substance. It sings, it prays, it proclaims, yet it does not obey. This is the reverence that comforts the conscience without transforming the heart.

To say “Lord” while choosing one’s own path is to build a temple without a throne. It is devotion without submission—light without fire. For the true weight of the word “Lord” is not carried by speech, but by surrender.

The Hidden Throne

Every soul has a throne. It is the seat from which decisions flow, desires are approved, and actions are born. No matter how many titles are spoken outwardly, the one who sits upon that inner throne is the true lord of the life.

Many invite the Divine into their lives as a guest—respected, acknowledged, even honored—but never enthroned. But a guest does not command. A guest does not govern. A guest does not transform the house. So the question returns with greater clarity: if He is called Lord, why is He not obeyed?

The Cost of the Word

To call Him Lord is to accept the death of self-rule. It is to say: my understanding is not supreme, my desires are not final, my path is not ultimate. It is the yielding of the inner kingdom.

This is why many speak the word, yet resist its meaning—because the cost is not small. It requires the breaking of pride, the undoing of illusions, and the surrender of control. But what is lost in surrender is illusion. What is gained is truth.

Hearing and Doing

There are those who hear truth, and there are those who become it. The difference is not knowledge, but obedience. To hear and not do is to build on sand—impressive in appearance, but fragile in reality.

When the winds rise—and they will—what is not rooted in obedience cannot stand. But to hear and do is to align heaven and earth within the soul. It is to become a living testimony that the word “Lord” is not merely spoken, but embodied.

The Silent Answer

The question, “Why do you call Me Lord?” is not answered by explanation. It is answered by life. It is answered in the unseen choices: when obedience costs comfort, when truth confronts desire, and when surrender replaces control.

It is answered in the quiet, where no audience applauds and no recognition is given—only alignment.

Conclusion

This question is not meant to expose hypocrisy alone—it is also an invitation: to step out of the illusion of partial devotion and into the fullness of true surrender; to move from saying to being, from knowing to living, from calling to following.

For the One who asks the question does not seek titles. He seeks truth within the inward parts. To call Him Lord is to say, “Not my will, but Yours,” and to mean it—not once, but daily. In the end, the question is not whether the word was spoken, but whether it was lived.
